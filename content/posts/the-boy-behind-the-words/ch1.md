April 2014.

I was in the fourth semester of my B.Tech, staying in the hostel. I had just finished three papers when the rest of the exams were postponed because of the elections. I didn't waste a second—I went straight back home.

I had plenty of time to study, but instead, I was just bored. Thanks to my mother, who never left a stone unturned to make sure I didn't become "lazy and fat," I was constantly looking for a rescue.

And then came my rescue — Facebook.

To be honest, I had no idea what Facebook even was until my best friend created an account for me without asking. Somehow, I slowly started spending more and more time there.

Then one afternoon, while scrolling aimlessly, I came across a post titled:

**Our Unfinished Years - Chapter 3: The House of Strangers**

I paused for a moment. *What kind of title is this?*

Out of curiosity, I opened it.

The writing pulled me in instantly. Every line carried emotion, loneliness, and a strange warmth hidden beneath pain. It felt honest in a way most things never do. I was living the same life as described in the story.

I was amazed.

So amazed, in fact, that I immediately searched for the earlier chapters:

- **Our Unfinished Years - Chapter 1: The Decision of a Nerd**  
- **Our Unfinished Years - Chapter 2: The Girl on the Night Bus**

I read them all in one sitting. It felt like reading a real author, someone who knew how to make words reach the heart.

When I finally checked the writer's profile, I was surprised to see that he was from my own college.

That made me curious.

I noticed he had mentioned a blog in his profile. I visited it and started reading more of his stories. Before long, it became part of my daily routine.

Funny, but true — in the beginning, I was not interested in the writer. I was interested in the writing.

After all, a person is known by their work, right?

Days passed, and I got busy with my studies. 

Occasionally, I used to check if there was a new chapter published. But, no update for a long time. 

I used to receive many friend requests, and I usually ignored most of them. Some came from strangers. Some of the boys seemed to send requests to every girl they saw. But when I saw his name, I paused. We had many mutual friends, including my best friend.

He was from my college, and by then I already knew him in another way — through his words. Maybe the mutual friends made it easier. For some reason I still cannot explain, I felt unexpectedly happy. Excited, even. I accepted it immediately. Even now, I wonder why I felt so happy. I even clicked the message button. Then I stopped.

Why should I message him first? He sent the request; he could start the conversation. That sounded sensible enough. I didn't want to look too eager.

So I closed the screen.

Five minutes later, I opened Facebook again. No message.
Ten minutes later, I checked again. Still nothing.

That became my routine. Every few minutes: check for a message, check if he's online. Nothing. Two days passed, but they felt much longer.

Then one evening, my phone finally pinged.

A message from him. 

I just stared at the screen for a second. It was casual—a five-minute conversation that ended almost as soon as it started. But those five minutes stayed with me all night.

I expected another message the next day. Nothing. Absolutely nothing. Maybe I was expecting too much. He didn't know I wanted to talk to him.

I eventually returned to the hostel. The day before my final exam, he messaged again. Finally, he seemed more worried about my paper than I was, insisting we talk properly after I was done.

That made me happy. I finally told him that I liked his stories—all of them.

He was surprised. We talked for an hour. From that day on, we were friends. He wrote, I read, we discussed.

I liked talking to him, but only as a friend. My heart already belonged somewhere else.

At least, that is what I believed then.
