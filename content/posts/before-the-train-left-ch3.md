Some people introduce themselves with names.

Others introduce themselves with silences.

She looked at me for several seconds after I said, Try me. It was not the ordinary pause of someone deciding what to say. It felt like she was measuring whether I was worth the effort of honesty.

The station around us continued its restless movement. Families shifted luggage. Vendors shouted over one another. A child cried because he wanted a balloon. Somewhere a tea glass shattered, followed by an argument and laughter.

Yet between us there was a pocket of stillness.

“I don’t know if I should say this to you,” she said at last.

“You can say it,” I replied. “We are not strangers anymore.”

The sentence sounded bold when it left my mouth. Inside, I was praying it did not sound ridiculous.

She tilted her head slightly. “Not strangers?”

“After an eight-minute conversation?” I said. “No. At least not complete strangers.”

She almost smiled.

“You think too quickly.”

“And you think too slowly.”

That earned me a proper smile.

“Fine,” she said. “Then maybe we should begin correctly.”

“Yes,” I said, suddenly aware of how little I knew. “Your name.”

“Asmita.”

The name settled into me immediately, as if I had been waiting for it long before hearing it.

“Asmita,” I repeated.

“And yours?”

For reasons I still cannot explain, hearing her ask my name felt more intimate than anything else that had happened so far.

“Ronak”

She nodded once, as if confirming something private to herself.

“What?”

“Nothing,” she said. “Ronak suits you.”

“How does a name suit someone?”

“It just does.”

“That sounds like something invented on the spot.”

“Maybe.” She shrugged. “Maybe I invent many things.”

At the time, I thought she was joking.

I would later learn that some jokes arrive early as warnings.

“So,” I said, “now that we know names, can I know why Asmita looks like she wants to fight the entire world?”

She laughed softly.

“I don’t want to fight the world.”

“Then only half of it?”

“Maybe my family.”

The answer came so plainly that it startled me.

“What happened?”

She adjusted the strap of her bag and looked toward the tracks before speaking.

“You know the biggest problem a girl has after a certain age?”

“Exams?”

She gave me a look.

“That was a terrible answer.”

“I know. I panicked.”

“Marriage,” she said. “That becomes everyone’s favorite topic.”

I nodded carefully, not sure where this was leading.

“My father wanted me to become a doctor,” she continued. “I prepared for medical entrance. I failed.”

There was no self-pity in the way she said it. Just fact.

“Then I joined engineering because that was the next respectable dream available.”

“Which college?”

“Vignan.”

“And?”

“And I hated every day of it.”

The honesty of the statement made me laugh before I could stop myself.

She looked offended for half a second, then laughed too.

“I’m serious.”

“So am I,” I said. “I was laughing because you said it like a court judgment.”

“It was a punishment.”

“What branch?”

“Electrical.”

“That sounds dangerous.”

“It is, especially for people with no interest in electricity.”

I liked the way she spoke—sharp, quick, without trying to impress.

“So what do you want instead?” I asked.

She grew quieter.

That change in her was becoming familiar: playful one moment, distant the next.

“I want to paint.”

“Paint walls?”

She stared at me.

“I’m joking,” I said quickly.

“You are not as funny as you think.”

“I’m discovering that.”

“I mean painting,” she said. “Canvas. Paper. Nature. Faces. Light. Things that disappear if you don’t capture them.”

Something in her voice changed when she spoke of it. Until then, she had been interesting. Now she became alive.

“I lose myself when I paint,” she continued. “Hours pass. I forget hunger. I forget time. I forget everyone telling me what I should become.”

The station noise returned faintly to my ears, but it felt far away.

“And your family?”

“They think painting is a hobby.”

“And you?”

“I think breathing is a hobby compared to it.”

That line stayed with me for years.

“So what now?” I asked.

“They gave me three months.”

“To paint?”

“To prove I can make something of myself. Otherwise…” She looked away.

“Otherwise marriage.”

She nodded.

“With someone chosen for you?”

“With someone suitable.” Her voice carried enough sarcasm to answer the question.

I did not know what advice to give. I was only a boy at a railway station pretending to be wiser than he was.

Still, I said the first honest thing that came to mind.

“That’s unfair.”

“They’re worried,” she said. “Parents call fear by many beautiful names.”

I had never heard anyone my age speak like that.

“You sound older than you look.”

“And you sound younger than you think.”

“That was rude.”

“That was accurate.”

I should have been offended. Instead, I smiled.

There was something dangerous about being understood too quickly.

“So what are you doing now?” I asked.

“Taking a break.”

“By traveling?”

“By breathing somewhere else for two days.”

I laughed.

“That’s not a plan.”

“It is a perfect plan.”

“You really believe someone will rescue you?”

She looked at me with a softness I had not seen before.

“No one rescues anyone,” she said. “But sometimes a person arrives and reminds you that escape is possible.”

I wanted to ask what kind of person she meant.

I wanted to ask whether she believed in destiny.

I wanted to ask whether she noticed how fast my heartbeat had become since learning her name.

Before I could speak, a long train horn cut through the evening air.

Her expression changed instantly.

She turned toward the tracks.

Lights were approaching.

“My train,” she said.

And suddenly every second on Platform Number One became precious.
