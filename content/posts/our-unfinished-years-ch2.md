Before classes began, I prepared myself for the reason I had come to Berhampur.

My uniform had been stitched. My bag was packed. I had memorized the syllabus as if classes would begin with an examination. I still had two days left before college opened, but my mind had already reached there.

I had not informed my elder brother that I was coming.

I knew only the area where he stayed—Mandiapalli, near NH5. I had visited once before during the diploma entrance examination conducted by NTTF. I had secured a rank, but I never cared much for it. My mind was already set on another road.

My father booked me a ticket on the Lizza bus, scheduled to leave at eleven that night.

He dropped me at the bus stand and waited until I boarded. Fathers often say little during departures. Their silence carries enough.

I took the window seat.

That had always been my habit. Like most boys, I believed the window belonged to dreamers. I expected a long quiet journey with cold wind, darkness outside, and thoughts inside.

But five minutes after the bus started, someone stopped beside my seat.

A young woman placed her bag overhead and sat next to me.

She had curly hair, kind eyes, and the kind of smile that made strangers feel less strange.

“Where are you going?” she asked casually.

I turned toward her in surprise.

“Yes?”

She laughed softly.

“I asked where your journey ends.”

“Oh. Berhampur.”

“Same as mine,” she said. “I’m Alisha. And you?”

“Ronak… Ronak Bharadwaj.”

“For studies?”

“Yes. +2.”

“Good,” she said. “I will not bore you with marks, percentages, and career advice.”

I felt relieved.

“But I can still ask one thing,” she continued. “What do you want to become?”

I thought for a moment.

“I don’t really know.”

Then my tongue betrayed me.

“Still figuring it out, didi.”

The word slipped out naturally.

I had no sister, yet something about her presence made it feel right.

She raised an eyebrow.

“So now that you have called me didi, I have the right to scold you.”

I smiled nervously.

“You should know what you want from your own life.”

“Then tell me,” I replied, gathering courage. “What do you want?”

She leaned back proudly.

“I study economics. But journalism is my dream.”

“Really? How can you be so sure?”

“You listen to your heart,” she said. “Then you keep listening even when others talk louder.”

Her words were simple, but they carried weight.

“And you?” she asked again.

I hesitated.

“Maybe… programming.”

“But software life is difficult.”

“Everyone says that,” I replied. “Still, I like it.”

She nodded.

“That matters more.”

Then she noticed the book in my hand.

“What are you reading?”

“Peter Pan,” I said, lifting it proudly. “The story by J. M. Barrie.”

She smiled.

“I read it when I was a girl.”

“Meaning?”

She pretended offense.

“Meaning I am a lady now.”

I quickly looked away.

She laughed again.

Then she asked, “Do you write?”

“Write? No.”

“People who read deeply often write someday.”

“Maybe,” I said, though I did not believe it.

A short silence followed.

Then she gently took the book from my hand, closed it, and returned it.

“Enough reading. Sleep now.”

I checked my watch.

12:15 AM.

“Yes, Di. Good night.”

The word Di came easier than Didi.

Something about shortening it made it warmer.

“Good night,” she said, wrapping her shawl around herself.

I looked out of the window.

The road outside was endless blackness, broken only by passing lights. Somewhere between those shadows and the hum of the bus, I fell asleep.

I woke to a sweet voice.

“Berhampur.”

I opened my eyes slowly.

There she was, gathering her things.

“Already?” I asked, half asleep.

“Yes, sahibzade,” she said playfully.

I quickly packed my bag.

“Where will you get down?” she asked.

“Old bus stand. I need to take another bus toward Chhatrapur.”

“Why?”

“I’m staying with my brother in Mandiapalli.”

She shook her head.

“No. Get down at State Bank Square instead.”

Then she held out her hand.

“Give me your phone.”

“My phone?”

“Just give it.”

I handed it over obediently.

She typed quickly, pressed a few buttons, and returned it.

The bus slowed.

“Your stop.”

I hurried down with my bag and turned back to say goodbye.

But before I could speak, the bus had already begun moving.

Through the window, I saw her smiling.

I could not hear her clearly, but I understood her lips.

I saved my number. Call if you need anything. I live near New Bus Stand. And thank you… I always wanted a younger brother.

Then the bus disappeared into the waking city.

I stood there alone with my bag.

Morning light had begun to spread across the streets.

I opened my phone.

A new contact had been saved.

**My Sweet Di**

I smiled to myself.

In a city where I knew no one, I had already found family.
