A few weeks later, she invited me to work with her from a café.

Not badminton.

Not a game booking.

Just work.

That difference alone made the invitation feel unusually personal.

She said she would be working from Third Wave Coffee at Nexus Shantiniketan and asked if I wanted to join.

I tried replying normally.

But internally, my brain had already converted the entire day into an event.

The strange thing about liking someone is how quietly it changes ordinary things. Suddenly, choosing a T-shirt takes longer. You check your reflection more than necessary. You start planning travel time carefully as if punctuality itself carries emotional meaning.

And underneath all of that preparation sits one embarrassing truth:

You are excited just to exist near them.

At one point, I casually suggested we could go together.

She immediately said no.

Not rudely.

Just directly.

She preferred meeting there.

That small refusal stayed with me during the drive.

Not painfully. Just enough to remind me that whatever this was inside my head, she was still maintaining her own boundaries naturally.

By the time I reached the mall, I had already called her once because I couldn’t properly find the café entrance.

When I finally entered, she was already there.

Sitting with her laptop open.

Working.

And before anything else, she smiled slightly and told me something with visible satisfaction in her voice.

“I reserved this seat for you.”

The chair beside her was empty.

Saved.

I don’t think she realized how disproportionately important that felt to me.

Because until then, most of our interactions had been temporary. Badminton courts. Short conversations. Random timings. But this felt intentional. Like she had expected me beside her before I even arrived.

And strangely, she looked genuinely happy about it.

Almost proud.

As if I was supposed to appreciate the effort.

And I did.

Probably more than she knew.

She explained why she liked working there. The noise helped her focus somehow. People around her created enough distraction to stop overthinking. She spoke casually while opening tabs on her laptop, adjusting cables, arranging things around her space like someone already familiar with the routine.

After some time, the food she had ordered arrived.

It was already lunch time by then.

I didn’t order anything.

She didn’t offer either.

And somehow that detail stayed with me too.

Not because I expected her to pay. But because attraction makes people notice tiny absences alongside tiny kindnesses.

We started talking slowly between her work.

Not continuously.

Just conversations appearing and disappearing naturally between notifications, calls, typing, and silence.

She talked about her family.

About home.

About random things connected to growing up.

At some point I realized she was speaking comfortably in Hindi without hesitation, and that surprised me. Not because she knew Hindi, but because the conversation had quietly crossed the formal stage where people carefully stay inside English.

It felt more relaxed now.

More lived-in.

At one point she casually mentioned how safe it felt to leave laptops and belongings there while going around the mall.

I remember looking at our things lying openly on the table and realizing she trusted places more easily than I did.

Or maybe she trusted life more easily than I did.

Then she asked about my housewarming ceremony.

After that, unexpectedly, she asked about marriage proposals.

Whether my family was searching.

Whether I was looking for someone to date.

The question arrived so casually that I almost missed its weight.

I told her I wasn’t sure.

That answer opened another conversation entirely.

Before talking about herself, she suddenly asked:

“You don’t have caste issues, right?”

I remember pausing slightly at that.

Not because I was offended.

But because the question felt like it came from somewhere deeper than casual discussion.

And then she started talking about him.

Her boyfriend.

Not directly at first.

But slowly the shape of the story became clear.

They had caste problems.

Family complications.

An uncertain future hanging over the relationship.

And while she spoke, I noticed something change in her expression.

Not sadness exactly.

Something heavier.

Like someone discussing a problem they had already spent too many nights thinking about alone.

I listened quietly.

And somewhere in the middle of her talking, an uncomfortable realization settled inside me.

Until then, her boyfriend had existed mostly as an incomplete idea in my head.

An assumption.

A possibility.

But sitting there across from her while she spoke about him seriously made everything feel real in a way I had been subconsciously avoiding.

Real relationships.

Real problems.

Real emotional history.

Not the half-imagined space my mind had secretly been living inside.
