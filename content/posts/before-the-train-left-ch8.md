There are seasons in life that last only days.

Yet when they end, they feel longer and more real than entire years.

The two weeks that followed her first message became one of those seasons.

After the shock of discovering that **Cute Princess Asmita (Priya)** was *the* Asmita, sleep became a negotiable thing. Routine became optional. Time itself began arranging around one simple possibility:

*Would she come online tonight?*

At first, our conversations were careful.

Two strangers pretending they were not curious about each other.

She asked ordinary questions in extraordinary ways.

“What are you doing?”

“Breathing,” I replied.

“Waste of talent.”

“And you?”

“Improving the world.”

“How?”

“By talking to difficult people.”

That was how she spoke—half playful, half impossible to decode.

I never knew whether to laugh, argue, admire, or surrender.

Usually I did all four.

## The New Ritual

Days became waiting rooms.

Mornings belonged to family, chores, and whatever responsibilities I was expected to remember.

Afternoons passed in distraction.

Evenings slowed unbearably.

And nights—nights belonged to Facebook.

I learned the sound of a new message before it appeared. I learned to keep one eye on the chat sidebar while pretending to do other things. I learned that five minutes without a reply could feel like betrayal, while one smiling emoji could repair an entire day.

She arrived online unpredictably.

Some nights at 9:15.

Some nights at 11:40.

Some nights not at all.

Those were the longest nights.

## Knowing Her

She never gave information in straight lines.

If I asked, “What did you do today?”

She might answer:

“Argued with my mother, drank tea, saw a cloud shaped like a rabbit, decided not to become ordinary.”

Or:

“Nothing worth recording. What color shirt are you wearing?”

“How is that related?”

“It isn’t. Answer.”

Sometimes I wondered if she enjoyed confusing me more than talking to me.

Sometimes I knew she did.

Yet slowly, through jokes and detours, I began to know her.

She loved rain but hated umbrellas.

She preferred sketching faces to landscapes.

She judged people by how they spoke to waiters.

She became quiet when family was mentioned too deeply.

She laughed often—but only when something genuinely amused her.

And she had the rare ability to make attention feel like affection.

## Knowing Me

Worse still, she began to know me.

Too quickly.

“You overthink before sending messages,” she wrote one night.

“No, I don’t.”

“You deleted three versions before this reply.”

I stared at the screen.

“How would you know that?”

“Because this sentence sounds edited.”

“You are impossible.”

“You are transparent.”

Another night:

“You act confident only in text.”

“That is false.”

“If I meet you tomorrow, you will say hello and then stare at the floor.”

I hated how accurate that sounded.

“You think you understand everyone?”

“No,” she replied. “Only you.”

That message stayed in my mind for hours.

## The Invisible Dependency

No one around me knew anything had changed.

At home, I still sat in the same chair. Ate at the same table. Answered the same questions.

But internally, my world had acquired weather.

If she was warm, the day brightened.

If she was distant, everything dimmed.

If she praised me casually, I replayed it.

If she disappeared mid-conversation, I checked my inbox ten times.

I began measuring self-worth through typing indicators.

This is how foolishness enters politely.

## The First Jealousy

One night she replied late.

“Sorry,” she wrote. “Was chatting with a friend.”

The sentence should have meant nothing.

Instead, I felt a sharp discomfort I had no right to feel.

“Which friend?”

“Why?”

“Just asking.”

“Boy or girl matters to you?”

“No.”

“Liar.”

I denied it repeatedly.

She laughed repeatedly.

That was the first time I realized attachment had moved ahead of permission.

## Her Soft Side

She was not always sharp.

Sometimes, near midnight, another Asmita appeared.

Less playful. More honest.

“Do you ever feel lonely in a crowded house?”

she asked once.

“Yes.”

“How?”

“Because noise is not the same as connection.”

There was a long pause.

Then:

“Good answer.”

Another night:

“What if we become strangers later?”

I typed quickly.

“We won’t.”

She replied after a minute.

“You say many things with confidence.”

I wanted to ask why that sounded sad.

I didn’t.

## My Mistake

I began assuming continuity.

I thought because we spoke daily, we would keep speaking daily.

I thought because she laughed with me, I understood her.

I thought because I missed her, she must miss me the same way.

Youth often mistakes intensity for certainty.

## The Last Bright Night

Toward the end of those two weeks, we spoke for nearly three hours.

About school memories.

About dreams.

About why some people feel familiar too soon.

About whether destiny existed.

She wrote:

“Maybe some meetings are planned.”

“By whom?” I asked.

“By boredom,” she said first.

Then after a pause:

“By something bigger.”

When we finally said goodnight, I sat smiling at the blank screen long after she had gone offline.

I believed I was entering something beautiful.

I did not know I was standing at the edge of confusion.

Because light is most dangerous just before it fades.
