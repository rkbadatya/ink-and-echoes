Some moments are too small for the world to notice.

A click of a button.
A single word in a chat box.
A green dot beside a name.

And yet those moments can divide a life more sharply than grand events.

The friend request arrived at 10:15 PM.

I had been online for nearly an hour, pretending not to wait for anything. Several chat windows were open. Friends were discussing plans, jokes, cricket scores, and matters that usually interested me. That night they felt like background noise.

Then the notification appeared.

**1 Friend Request**

Without thinking, I clicked **Confirm** immediately and returned to another conversation.

It meant nothing at first.

People sent requests all the time—schoolmates, mutual friends, forgotten classmates, random names trying to increase their friend lists. I approved it automatically, barely glancing at the sender.

Ten minutes later, a message box opened.

**hey**

I looked at the name attached to it.

**Cute Princess Asmita (Priya)**

For a moment my body forgot every function except staring.

The room became silent.

The fan was still spinning. The computer was still humming. Friends were still messaging in other windows.

But everything had moved far away.

I clicked her profile again to make sure.

Baby picture. Hidden details. Same name.

It was her.

The girl from Platform Number One.

The girl who had left with a smile and an impossible instruction.

The girl I had spent one sleepless night searching for.

She had found me.

I placed both hands on the keyboard and typed:

**hi**

Then deleted it.

Typed again:

**Hello Asmita :)**

Deleted that too.

Too eager.

Typed:

**Hi**

Sent.

I leaned back immediately, disgusted by my own lack of creativity.

Three dots appeared.

She was typing.

Then came the message:

**So you got the solution to my problem?**

I blinked twice.

No greeting.

No formality.

No “How are you?”

Straight to confusion.

**Which problem?** I wrote.

Her reply came instantly.

**Short-term memory loss? Long-term memory loss?**

I stared at the screen.

Then understanding struck like electricity.

**Wait… are you Asmita from the station??**

**No, I am the ticket collector.**

I laughed aloud.

**Yes, of course I’m Asmita. Took you long enough.**

My fingers moved faster now.

**How did you find me? I searched everywhere!**

**Everywhere? Impressive. But badly done.**

**That is rude.**

**That is accurate.**

Already she sounded exactly the same.

Sharp. Playful. Slightly dangerous.

I could almost hear her voice through the text.

**Seriously, how did you find me?**

There was a pause before she replied.

**I saw your college ID card when you were talking to me. Your name was visible. Then your profile picture helped.**

I sat back in shock.

All evening I had thought of myself as the observer, the one noticing every detail.

Meanwhile, she had noticed more.

**So you were spying on me?**

**Girls call it observing. Boys call it spying when they lose.**

I smiled helplessly.

She continued:

**Anyway, you should thank me.**

**For what?**

**You forgot to ask my surname.**

I covered my face with one hand.

Even through a screen she had managed to embarrass me.

**I was going to ask.**

**The train saved you from lying.**

**You enjoy insulting people, don’t you?**

**Only selected people. Feel honored.**

That line gave me more happiness than it should have.

## The First Real Conversation

What followed was not dramatic.

No declarations. No destiny speeches.

Just conversation.

But conversation has its own intimacy when two people are unexpectedly glad the other exists.

She asked about my brother.

I asked if she had reached safely.

She mocked my nervousness at the station.

I defended myself badly.

She asked what I thought of her first.

I refused to answer.

She guessed correctly anyway.

**You thought I was beautiful.**

**I never said that.**

**You didn’t need to.**

**You are overconfident.**

**And you are easy to read.**

I hated how true that felt.

Hours slipped past unnoticed.

The house slept around me.

My room remained lit by the old monitor glow.

At some point I realized I was smiling continuously for no reason.

## Before Logging Off

Near midnight, she wrote:

**Good. You are less boring online than at the station.**

**That sounds like half a compliment.**

**Take what you get.**

Then, after a pause:

**Goodnight, Ronak.**

Again, seeing my name from her changed something in me.

**Goodnight, Asmita.**

She went offline.

The green dot vanished.

The room became ordinary again.

But not me.

I sat there staring at the blank chat window.

A few hours earlier she had been a lost possibility buried inside hundreds of search results.

Now she was real again.

Reachable.

Typing somewhere in the same night.

And though I did not yet understand it, something dangerous had begun.

Not love.

Not even closeness.

Something smaller, faster, and often more powerful:

anticipation.
