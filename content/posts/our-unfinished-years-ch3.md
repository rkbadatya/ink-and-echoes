I reached State Bank Square and ended up waiting longer than I expected.

The morning was still young, half-asleep itself. Trucks roared past like they owned the road, and government buses stopped and left without caring who was watching. A few people stood scattered around with bags on their shoulders, each one lost in their own journey. Even the street dogs looked like they had nowhere urgent to be.

I, on the other hand, had nowhere but time.

Sleep kept pulling at me. I was not used to mornings like this—definitely not before sunrise. Back home, this hour belonged to blankets and silence, not standing on cold concrete pretending to be awake. I already missed home more than I expected: my bed, the smell of food, and the comfort of routine that I never appreciated until I left it.

Finally, a white Tata mini bus arrived with a tired engine sound.

I didn’t think twice. I got in and asked the conductor if it was going toward Mandiapalli. He nodded like it was the simplest question in the world.

That was enough.

Inside, I squeezed myself into a window seat and held my bag tightly on my lap. The air from the cracked window was cold and honest. I told myself I would stay awake this time, just to watch the city pass by properly.

I didn’t even last ten minutes.

Somewhere between motion and silence, I fell asleep.

In my dream, I was home.

My mother was feeding me rice, dal, and potato fry with the kind of care only mothers know how to give. She was also doing her usual lecture—study well, don’t eat outside, listen to your brother, wake up early. I nodded like I was paying attention, even though I wasn’t.

On the television, *The Matrix* was playing. We had recently bought a DVD player, and I was in that phase where movies felt like life itself.

Then, strangely, even the movie seemed to change.

“MANDIAPALLI! MANDIAPALLI!”

I frowned in my dream.

Why is even Hollywood shouting my stop?

The bus jerked hard.

And I woke up.

Reality returned in the form of the conductor standing beside me, still shouting the stop name like a ritual.

I got off the bus feeling slightly betrayed by physics.

The vehicle was packed so tightly that I briefly wondered if humans were meant to fit like this at all. The conductor made it sound routine.

“Pass your bag through the window first,” he said casually.

“And come out the same way.”

So I did exactly that.

Bag first. Then me. No questions asked.

I paid five rupees and stepped out like someone who had survived a minor experiment.

Crossing NH5 was another story altogether.

Every time I thought the road was clear, something massive would appear—truck, bus, or speeding bike—like the road itself was testing my patience. After several failed attempts and a lot of hesitation, I finally made it across.

It was barely six-thirty in the morning.

A small shop nearby had just opened. I bought cake and biscuits and started walking toward my brother’s place, eating breakfast like someone who had escaped from sleep and responsibility at the same time.

Soon I saw it.

A simple rented house. Shared. Messy. Alive.

We used to call it a “mesh house”—a place where students lived together without really planning how life should be organized.

I knocked.

No response.

I knocked again.

Still nothing.

After a while, the door finally opened.

My elder brother stood there in a half-awake state—hair uncombed, eyes confused, expression slowly shifting from irritation to recognition.

“What the—how are you here?”

Before I could answer, more questions followed.

“Who brought you? Why didn’t you tell me? Since when are you here? Why so early? What happened?”

I let him finish.

Then I simply said, “Water first.”

That stopped everything.

He stared at me for a second, then silently handed me a bottle.

I drank it slowly while he kept looking at me like I was a surprise delivery he didn’t order.

“I’m tired,” I finally said. “I need sleep.”

That was the end of interrogation.

He gave me a shawl, pointed to the bed, and I collapsed almost instantly.

Somewhere in the background, I heard him calling my father.

I didn’t care.

Sleep won.

When I woke up, it was already past ten.

Lunch was ready. My brother had cooked.

After freshening up, I ate like someone who hadn’t seen food in days. Only then did I actually look around properly.

The room was small but surprisingly disciplined. Books arranged neatly. Clothes folded. Utensils stacked properly. Even a small temple sat quietly in one corner, as if reminding everyone that chaos had limits.

I offered a silent prayer before eating.

Then my brother explained everything—college timings, daily routine, expenses, bus routes, survival rules of student life in Berhampur. It all sounded like a different world.

After that, he introduced me to his roommates.

Narayan Bhai. Subham. Tanish Bhai.

All of them older, louder, more confident—already living a life I had just stepped into.

One person was still missing.

I tried to stay quiet, adjusting into the background.

Then he arrived.

Smiling. Energetic. Completely at ease.

Before I could react, he hugged me.

“You must be the wonder boy,” he said loudly. “Your brother talks about you like the whole college knows you.”

I had no idea how to respond to that kind of introduction.

So I just said, “Hmm.”

He laughed.

“I’m Kartik. Call me Kartik Bhai. People think I’m sixteen sometimes.”

That broke something in the room. Everyone laughed.

And I smiled too—properly, for the first time that day.

It was Sunday. They had plans for a college picnic. I wasn’t part of that world yet, and nobody pretended otherwise.

I told them I’d explore the city and come back in the evening.

Soon they left, noisy and full of energy, leaving the house behind like it was just a pause in their life.

And then it became silent.

Completely silent.

A new city.

A strange house.

People I didn’t know.

No parents.

No familiar voices.

Just walls, books, and time stretching endlessly in front of me.

I sat on the bed for a long time without moving.

And somewhere in that silence, one thought arrived clearly.

I loved my home.
