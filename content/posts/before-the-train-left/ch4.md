There are departures that happen in front of your eyes.

And there are departures that begin inside you long before the train moves.

The moment she said, My train, something tightened in my chest.

Until then, time had felt generous. The station clock existed somewhere above us, unnoticed and unimportant. Minutes had passed like conversation always wishes they would—quickly enough to feel effortless, slowly enough to feel endless.

Now every second became visible.

The approaching train announced itself with a growing thunder beneath the rails. Heads turned all across the platform. People rose from benches, gathered bags, called out to relatives, straightened shawls, lifted children. Vendors moved aside. Porters became urgent.

The ordinary chaos of travel returned in full force.

And in the middle of it, Asmita seemed suddenly farther away than when I had first seen her.

She adjusted her bag and stepped closer to the edge of the platform, eyes fixed on the incoming coaches.

I stood beside her, uselessly silent.

There are many speeches people imagine themselves giving in important moments. Confident speeches. Memorable speeches. Honest speeches.

Reality usually gives you none of them.

"So," she said without looking at me, "looks like your rescue mission is over."

"I didn't rescue anyone."

"You listened."

"That counts?"

"Sometimes more than advice."

I wanted to tell her that I had done almost nothing, that she had given me more in thirty minutes than I had offered in return. She had turned a routine afternoon into something I knew I would remember for years.

Instead I said, "You still haven't solved anything."

She smiled faintly. "Neither have you."

The train rolled in with metal shrieks and bursts of warm air. Windows flashed past us. Faces appeared and vanished behind bars and glass. Some passengers leaned out before the train had fully stopped, impatient to arrive before arrival itself.

People surged forward.

Asmita moved with them.

I followed a step behind, afraid of appearing foolish, more afraid of losing sight of her.

When her coach halted, she placed one hand on the handle near the entrance and turned back toward me.

For a brief second the crowd blurred around us.

"Ronak," she said.

It was the first time she had spoken my name.

I cannot explain why that mattered so much.

"Yes?"

"I hope you figure out a solution to my problem."

"How can I?" I said, trying to smile. "Even if I do, I have no way to tell you."

"You'll figure that out too."

"That is not a practical answer."

"You don't seem practical."

"That is not a compliment."

"It wasn't meant to be."

Even then she had the ability to confuse insult and affection until they sounded the same.

She placed one foot on the train step.

Panic arrived.

This was the moment. The last chance. If I let her leave now, she would become one more strange memory attached to a railway platform. A story I would tell myself later and improve with imagination.

I swallowed.

"At least give me your number," I said. "So I can send this life-changing advice."

She looked at me with open amusement.

"No need."

"No need?"

"You can send me a friend request."

The words landed beautifully—and uselessly.

"A friend request?"

She nodded.

"On Facebook."

"Of course on Facebook. Where else?"

I opened my mouth to ask the obvious question—what is your full name?—but at that exact moment someone behind her pushed luggage forward, someone else shouted for space, and the train jerked slightly.

She climbed fully inside.

Then she leaned out near the doorway, green ribbon stirring in the wind.

"Bye, Ronak."

"Bye… Asmita."

The train began to move.

Slowly first. Then steadily.

I walked beside it for a few steps like people do when they are unwilling to accept motion. She was still there at the doorway, one hand gripping the rail, smiling in that unreadable way of hers.

Then coach by coach, she passed beyond me.

The ribbon vanished.

The windows blurred.

The last carriage disappeared into evening.

And I was left standing on Platform Number One with two discoveries.

First: I already missed her.

Second: I had forgotten to ask her surname.

I stared down the empty tracks in disbelief.

"Well done," I muttered to myself. "Brilliant."

All that courage, all that effort, all those rehearsed conversations—and I had managed to learn only one name shared by half the country.

Asmita.

No surname.

No phone number.

No college details except Vignan.

No certainty that Berhampur was even her city.

Just a first name, a green ribbon, and a smile sharp enough to stay in memory.

I turned from the tracks and began walking back through the crowd, half-dazed.

That was when I heard someone call my name.

"Ronak!"

I looked up.

Srihari was waving from farther down the platform, carrying a backpack and grinning as if nothing extraordinary had happened in the world.

Maybe for him, nothing had.

For me, the world had changed and then boarded a train.

I walked toward him trying to appear normal.

"Where were you?" he asked.

"Here," I said.

He glanced past me at the tracks. "Looking lost?"

"Something like that."

We began walking together.

He kept talking about schedules, Bhubaneswar, delays, relatives, ordinary things. I answered where required, nodded when needed, and heard almost none of it.

Because my mind remained behind me on the platform.

At the place where a stranger had become unforgettable.

At the place where I had been given an invitation with no address.

At the place where, without realizing it, I had stepped into the first chapter of a story that had not yet decided whether it was a love story or a mistake.
