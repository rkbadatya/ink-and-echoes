Sometimes the message you wait for most arrives after it has already become dangerous.

For days I had imagined hearing from her again.

I had rehearsed calm replies, clever replies, indifferent replies, forgiving replies. In some versions I acted mature. In others, mysterious. In several, I ignored her completely and allowed regret to educate her.

Reality gave me none of those performances.

At 12:07 AM, on the first minutes of the new year, her message sat glowing on the screen.

**Happy New Year 2014 :)**

Simple.

Light.

Almost cheerful.

As if nothing had happened between us.

As if unanswered messages did not exist.

As if silence could be folded neatly away and replaced with a smiley face.

I read it again.

Then again.

A strange conflict began inside me.

One part of me felt immediate relief. She had remembered me. She had written first. She had returned.

Another part, darker and louder, asked:

*Now? After all this time?*

The room around me pulsed with distant celebration. Somewhere outside, crackers burst and boys shouted into the street. My phone vibrated repeatedly with greetings from relatives and friends. The world was entering a new year.

I was entering an old argument.

I typed:

**Thanks. Happy New Year :)**

Deleted it.

Typed:

**Same to you.**

Deleted.

Typed nothing.

Closed the chat window.

Opened it again.

I hated how much power one line of text still had over me.

## The Delay

I decided not to reply immediately.

That felt important.

Let her wait once.

Let her wonder.

Let her feel uncertainty in some small proportion.

So I placed both hands away from the keyboard and counted to thirty like a man controlling explosives.

At fifteen seconds, I reopened the message.

At twenty-two, I checked whether she was still online.

At thirty, I typed:

**Thanks. Happy New Year… :)**

I sent it before pride could object.

Then I stared at the screen.

Nothing.

No typing bubble.

No reply.

No instant continuation.

Just the quiet cruelty of my own expectation.

I laughed once, bitterly.

Even now, I was arranging hope faster than reality could disappoint it.

## The Return of Memory

After sending the reply, I should have moved on.

Instead, I sat there as midnight deepened and remembered everything at once.

The station.

The train.

The first chat.

Her teasing.

The nights of waiting.

The "seen" timestamps.

The feeling of being slowly erased without explanation.

I realized something uncomfortable:

I had not been angry only because she ignored me.

I had been angry because I had needed her not to.

Dependence always feels nobler when renamed affection.

## Her Next Message

At 12:19 AM, a new message arrived.

**Awake so late?**

I stared in disbelief.

That was her opening?

Not apology.

Not explanation.

Not even acknowledgment of the silence.

Just casual curiosity, as if we had spoken yesterday.

I typed sharply:

**People stay awake on New Year's.**

Sent.

A moment later:

**Hmm. You sound angry.**

I almost laughed.

Almost.

**Do I?**

**Yes. Also dramatic.**

There she was again—light where I was heavy, playful where I was wounded.

The contrast made me angrier.

## The Hidden Wound

Sometimes rage is only grief wearing armor.

I wanted to ask where she had been.

Why she read messages and said nothing.

Why she could vanish and reappear as if people were shelves where she left objects temporarily.

But I did not know how to ask vulnerable questions.

So I asked hard ones.

**You remember how to message now?**

There was a pause.

Long enough for satisfaction to appear.

Then:

**Oh. So that is the issue.**

Issue.

As if my weeks of confusion were an office file.

**Forget it,** I typed.

**No. Say it.**

**Nothing to say.**

**Then why are you upset?**

Because I missed you.
Because I checked every day.
Because your silence made me foolish.

Instead I wrote:

**You disappeared.**

She replied quickly.

**I was busy.**

Three words.

Too small for the damage they were expected to repair.

## The Turning Point

I felt my chest tighten with all the sentences I had not sent before.

Every ignored message returned at once.

Every silent afternoon.

Every pointless refresh of the inbox.

Every attempt to look unaffected.

I knew I should close the chat.

I knew midnight conversations with wounded pride never end well.

Instead, I leaned closer to the screen.

The boy who had waited was still there.

But now he had found his voice.

And it was not going to be gentle.
