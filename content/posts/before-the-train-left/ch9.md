There are many modern ways to suffer.

Some are dramatic. Some are expensive. Some require terrible life choices.

And some arrive quietly in the corner of a chat window beside one cruel little word:

**Seen.**

Until then, our conversations had created their own rhythm.

She would appear unexpectedly.
Mock me immediately.
Ask impossible questions.
Disappear before I could understand half of what she meant.

I had grown used to that rhythm.

Worse—I had begun depending on it.

Every evening I logged in with casual urgency, pretending to myself that I was only checking messages. But my eyes always moved first to the same place.

Was she online?

Green dot.

No green dot.

Typing.

Offline.

Last active.

These tiny signals had become weather reports for my emotional life.

Then one afternoon, I sent her a message.

**What are you doing?**

Simple. Harmless. A message any normal person could send without consequence.

No reply.

I checked again twenty minutes later.

Still nothing.

At 1:30 PM the status changed.

**Seen at 1:30 PM**

That was all.

No answer.

No joke.

No sarcasm.

No explanation.

Just confirmation that my words had reached her and been judged unnecessary.

I stared at the screen longer than dignity permits.

Maybe she was busy.

Maybe someone called her away.

Maybe she intended to reply later.

Maybe I should calm down.

All reasonable thoughts.

I ignored them completely.

## The First Wait

By evening, I checked again.

Nothing.

I sent another message.

**Busy?**

No reply.

Later that night:

**Everything okay?**

No reply.

Then came the worst stage of waiting—the stage where imagination becomes your enemy.

Was she angry?

Had I said something wrong last night?

Was she talking to someone else?

Had she grown bored?

Had she realized I was more interesting as an accident than as a habit?

I reopened old chats and reread them like sacred texts searching for hidden mistakes.

One joke seemed too sharp.

One reply looked too eager.

One emoji now felt humiliating.

This is what attachment does: it turns memory into evidence.

## The New Routine

The next day I woke and checked Facebook before brushing my teeth.

Nothing.

I checked after breakfast.

Nothing.

At noon.

Nothing.

At 4:12 PM:

**Seen at 4:12 PM**

Again no reply.

The cruelty of it amazed me.

She was not absent.

She was present enough to ignore me properly.

I tried to act normal.

At lunch my mother asked for the salt twice before I heard her.

My brother asked why I was smiling at an empty plate.

"I'm not smiling."

"You were. Also you look miserable. Interesting combination."

I told him to mind his own business.

He said that confirmed everything.

## Ego vs Need

That evening I made a decision.

I would not message first again.

Self-respect mattered.

Dignity mattered.

Men had survived centuries without online replies.

At 8:40 PM I sent:

**You disappeared.**

Immediately regretted it.

No response.

At 10:05 PM:

**Fine. Ignore me.**

I deleted it before sending.

At 10:07 PM I sent:

**Goodnight.**

No response.

I hated myself.

## Withdrawal

Days changed shape.

I stopped enjoying conversations with friends because none of them were her.

Music became background noise.

Books required concentration I no longer owned.

Even cricket scores seemed less meaningful than whether a typing bubble would appear.

I began spending more time at home, less time outside.

My friends called. I made excuses.

My brother noticed.

"You're becoming boring," he said.

"I was always boring."

"No. Earlier you were naturally boring. Now it looks intentional."

I threw a pillow at him.

He laughed and left.

## The Addiction of Maybe

The problem was not silence.

The problem was possibility.

If she had blocked me, I could have healed.

If she had said goodbye, I could have hated her.

If she had explained, I could have understood.

Instead, she remained there—visible, reachable, unreadable.

Sometimes online for three minutes.

Sometimes active late at night.

Sometimes reading messages and leaving no trace except timestamps.

Hope survives longest in ambiguity.

## The Breaking Point

One night, after another unanswered message, I opened her profile.

Same baby picture.

Same hidden details.

Same mystery.

I stared at the screen and said aloud:

"What do you want from me?"

The profile, unhelpfully, said nothing.

Then I realized something more dangerous.

It was not about what she wanted.

It was about what I had started wanting from her.

Attention.

Response.

Presence.

Proof that those two weeks had meant something to both of us.

And I had never once asked whether she had promised any of it.

## Midnight Truth

Past midnight, I opened our old chats again.

There she was in every line—clever, warm, confusing, impossible.

I smiled despite the pain.

Then I closed the window.

For the first time, I understood that missing someone is not always noble.

Sometimes it is simply losing control of your own mind.

And somewhere, in another room, another house, another screen-lit night—

she was online.

But not with me.
