There are moments in life when you hear your own words and instantly wish they belonged to someone else.

The second she asked, "You came all the way here to ask me that?" I knew I had failed.

Not just failed—collapsed magnificently.

Out of every possible sentence I had rehearsed in my mind, out of all the clever openings and calm introductions, I had chosen the one question that made me sound like a lost passenger with no ability to read a timetable.

But she was smiling.

That saved me.

Not a loud laugh, not mockery, not impatience. Just a quiet smile that reached her eyes for a second and then disappeared, as if she had accidentally allowed it to escape.

I tried to recover some dignity.

"No," I said, attempting a tone far more confident than I felt. "I was wondering whether I am late… or the train is."

The line sounded better in my head.

Still, she looked amused.

She turned slightly toward the tracks again, then back at me. "And you thought I would know?"

"I thought," I said, "that you look like someone who knows things."

That surprised even me.

She lifted an eyebrow. "Do I?"

"Yes."

"What kind of things?"

I should have stopped there. Any sensible person would have taken the small victory and retreated. But something about her voice made caution impossible.

"Important things," I said. "Train timings. Hidden secrets. Why people look worried on railway platforms."

She glanced at me properly now, studying my face as if deciding whether I was foolish or entertaining.

"Maybe I am worried because a stranger is asking too many questions."

"Then I should apologize."

"You should."

"I'm sorry."

"For which part?"

"All of it."

That smile appeared again, this time longer.

The strange thing about certain conversations is how quickly they create their own world. Around us, porters shouted, vendors moved past with tea, children cried, announcements crackled through speakers—but none of it seemed to belong to the same place anymore.

She shifted the strap of her bag on her shoulder.

"So," she said, "if not train timings, what did you really want to ask?"

The truth rushed to the front of my mind all at once.

Your name. Where are you from? Why do you look familiar? Why do I feel like I have known you for years? Why do I suddenly care whether your train comes or not?

Instead, I said, "Nothing important."

"Liar."

The word was gentle, almost playful.

I laughed nervously. "You decided that very fast."

"I notice people quickly."

"That sounds dangerous."

"It is."

For the first time since approaching her, I forgot to be afraid.

"Then I should be careful."

"You should have been careful before walking up to me."

"Too late now."

"Yes," she said softly. "Too late now."

Something in the way she said it made the sentence feel larger than it was.

A gust of wind moved across the platform. Her ribbon loosened further, and a section of hair slipped forward over her shoulder. She brushed it back absentmindedly, looking down the tracks again.

I noticed then that the worry had returned to her face.

"You really are waiting for someone," I said.

"No."

"For a train, then."

"Yes."

"That sounded complicated."

"It is."

I wanted to ask more, but this time I hesitated. Her tone had changed. Beneath the playful sharpness was something tired.

To keep the conversation light, I said, "I had another theory."

"Oh?"

"I thought you were lost and trapped here, waiting for someone to rescue you."

She looked at me, then laughed quietly.

"Well," she said, "I am trapped."

"Here?"

"No. At home."

The answer was unexpected enough to silence me.

"And rescue?" I asked.

"That part may also be true." She looked toward the rails again. "Maybe one day someone will come and take me far away."

There are sentences that sound dramatic when spoken by most people.

From her, it sounded honest.

I forgot every prepared line, every practiced charm, every attempt to impress. All that remained was curiosity.

"How can someone be trapped in their own home?" I asked.

For the first time, she did not answer immediately.

The smile faded.

She lowered her eyes, and when she spoke, her voice was quieter than before.

"I don't know if you would understand."

The platform announcement began again overhead, naming trains and delays no one truly listened to.

I listened only to her.

"Try me," I said.

She looked at me then—not as a stranger, not as an interruption, but as if weighing whether I deserved to hear the truth.

And somewhere in the distance, a train horn sounded.
