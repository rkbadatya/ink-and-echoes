Some nights do not allow sleep.

They stretch themselves across the hours like unanswered questions, refusing to end until they have rearranged something inside you.

That night began with family voices, luggage, and celebration.

By the time Srihari and I reached Bhubaneswar, Rajdhani Express had already arrived. My elder brother stood near the coach entrance with two bags and the tired expression of someone who had traveled too far to be impressed by reunions. My mother would later say that no one smiled like me that evening because I was happiest to see him after so long.

She was wrong.

I was smiling for reasons I could not explain to anyone.

We returned home together. The house became noisy in the comforting way only family homes can become after someone returns from a long journey. Questions were asked before shoes were removed. Tea appeared without request. Neighbors somehow learned of the arrival within minutes. My brother narrated stories from the train, complained about food, exaggerated delays, and laughed at things no one else found funny.

I participated in all of it like an actor who knows his lines but not his role.

Inside, something else was happening.

Every few minutes, without warning, I would see her again.

The loose green ribbon.

The amused look after my foolish question.

The way she had said my name before boarding the train.

*Ronak.*

No one had ever spoken it like that before.

My brother noticed my distraction before anyone else.

"You look tired," he said while unpacking.

"Travel," I replied.

"You didn't travel that much."

"I'm preparing for future tiredness."

He laughed. "Still strange."

I shrugged and escaped to my room.

The clock read 10:47 PM.

Normally, after a day like that, sleep would have come easily. But the room felt smaller than usual. The walls seemed to hold too much silence. I sat on the bed, then stood up. I opened a book and read the same line three times. I switched on the television and muted it within minutes. I checked my phone though I knew there was nothing to check.

Then I lay down and closed my eyes.

Instant mistake.

The station returned at once.

I could hear the announcement speaker. I could smell tea and diesel. I could see her stepping onto the train.

I opened my eyes immediately.

"What is wrong with me?" I asked the ceiling.

No answer came.

At 11:25 PM I walked outside.

The lane beyond our house was cool and nearly empty. A few streetlights glowed in patient yellow circles. Somewhere a dog barked without commitment. The winter air carried the quiet confidence of midnight approaching.

I sat on the low cement boundary wall near the gate.

For the first time all day, I was alone with the truth.

I wanted to meet her again.

Not casually. Not if fate happened to arrange it. I wanted it with the stubborn urgency of a child wanting the toy just taken away.

And the absurdity of it made me angry.

I knew almost nothing about her.

People build attachments slowly—with time, shared memories, trust, repetition. I had built one in less than an hour beside a railway track.

What kind of person does that?

Apparently, me.

I replayed every line of our conversation, searching for clues I had missed.

Had she said where she lived?

No.

Did she mention friends?

No.

Any surname?

No.

Any clear detail useful for locating a human being in modern civilization?

No.

Only one sentence mattered now.

*You can send me a friend request.*

I laughed aloud in the empty lane.

"Brilliant," I said to myself. "Send a friend request to Asmita. Just Asmita. Why not search for Priya, Pooja, or Neha while you're at it?"

Someone touched my shoulder.

I turned sharply.

It was my brother.

He sat beside me without asking permission.

"You're definitely strange tonight," he said.

"I came for air."

"You came for confusion."

"I don't know what you mean."

"You never do when lying."

I said nothing.

He leaned back and looked at the road. "You know what I like about nights here?"

"What?"

"They don't force answers." He glanced at me. "Only honesty."

I stared ahead.

He waited.

That was his gift—never pushing hard enough to make silence defensive.

Finally I said, "Nothing happened."

He smiled. "Excellent. Then you're sitting outside in winter because nothing happened."

I laughed despite myself.

He stood up. "Keep your secrets if you want. But don't let one day steal your peace."

He started walking back toward the house, then turned.

"And if this mystery person has a name, at least tell me whether I should approve."

I froze.

He grinned and went inside.

Brothers are dangerous because they notice what parents miss.

I remained outside for another ten minutes, embarrassed and strangely lighter.

At midnight I returned to my room.

The computer waited on the desk.

Old desktop monitor. Slow startup. Louder than necessary fan. A machine that had never before seemed capable of changing destiny.

I pressed the power button.

The screen glowed awake.

If sleep would not come, then I would search.

Facebook loaded with the patience of an ancient government office.

Login.

Notifications.

Ignored.

Search bar.

My fingers hovered over the keyboard.

Then I typed the only thing I truly knew.

**Asmita**

Hundreds of names appeared.

I stared at them in disbelief.

There were Asmitas from Berhampur, Bhubaneswar, Bangalore, Delhi, colleges I had never heard of, profiles with flowers, film stars, cartoon babies, hidden faces, no faces.

A civilization of Asmitas.

Somewhere among them might be the girl from Platform Number One.

Or none of them.

I clicked the first profile.

Wrong.

Second.

Wrong.

Third.

Definitely wrong.

I leaned back in frustration.

The night had only just begun.
