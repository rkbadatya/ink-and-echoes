Some meetings arrive without warning.

They do not knock, they do not announce themselves, and they do not ask whether your life is ready to change. They simply appear in the middle of an ordinary day and leave you wondering, years later, how everything became different because of a few stolen minutes.

The day I met Asmita began like any other December afternoon.

I was going to Bhubaneswar to receive my elder brother, who was returning by Rajdhani Express. The train was expected around evening, and to reach in time I had to board the Vishakha Express from Berhampur. Nothing about the journey felt special then. It was a routine responsibility, the kind families hand over casually to the youngest son as if time belonged more to him than anyone else.

The winter sun had softened by the time I reached Berhampur railway station.

Platform Number One was alive in its usual chaos. Vendors moved through the crowd carrying tea kettles that breathed steam into the cool air. Porters in red shirts walked quickly with luggage balanced on their shoulders. Children tugged at their mothers’ hands. Somewhere, a radio played an old Hindi song through broken speakers. Above all of it came the metallic voice of announcements, half-heard and half-ignored.

I had arrived early.

There was still time before my train, and with nothing else to do, I began walking the length of the platform. I watched people the way lonely travelers often do—without interest, simply to pass the minutes. Faces came and went. Families argued over seats they did not yet own. Students leaned against pillars with bags slung carelessly across their shoulders. Old men folded newspapers into precise rectangles.

And then I saw her.

She stood a little away from the crowd, near a bench beneath a fading station sign. She was not trying to attract attention, yet attention gathered around her all the same. Some people possess that strange power without knowing it.

She looked worried.

Not frightened, not helpless—just impatient in the way someone becomes when time has suddenly become important. Every few seconds she checked her wristwatch, then looked toward the tracks, then back at the watch again, as if one of them might finally give her the answer she needed.

She wore a college bag over one shoulder. Her hair had been tied loosely with a green ribbon that had begun to slip free. A few strands moved in the breeze and touched her cheek before she brushed them away absentmindedly.

There was something familiar about her.

I had the uncomfortable feeling of having seen her somewhere before—perhaps in a classroom corridor, perhaps at a festival, perhaps only in imagination. My memory searched desperately for certainty and returned nothing.

I should have looked away.

Instead, I slowed my steps.

Then I stopped walking entirely.

I pretended to study the train timetable. I pretended to read advertisements pasted on the wall. I pretended to be interested in everything except the girl who had suddenly become the center of the station.

The truth was simpler.

I wanted to talk to her.

For most people, that desire would have been small and manageable. For me, it was a crisis. I had never been one of those boys who could begin conversations with strangers as if confidence came free with birth. Words did not arrive easily when I needed them. They hid. They waited until the moment had passed, then returned in brilliant sentences too late to be useful.

Still, I kept watching her.

What would I say?

Excuse me, are you waiting for the Vishakha Express?

No, too dull.

Hello, haven’t I seen you somewhere before?

Too dishonest.

You look worried. Can I help?

Too eager.

I rehearsed imaginary openings in my mind while my body refused to move.

Minutes passed like this.

My heart had begun beating with the seriousness of an examination result. It was absurd. She was only a girl waiting for a train, and I was only a boy waiting for his own. Yet something about the distance between us felt larger than the platform itself.

I sat on the bench nearest to her.

From there I could hear the soft tap of her sandal against the ground. I could smell the faint mix of shampoo and winter air whenever the breeze changed direction. I could also feel the complete stupidity of sitting so close to someone while lacking the courage to speak.

She remained unaware of me.

Or perhaps she had noticed everything and chosen to ignore it.

Another announcement echoed overhead. A goods train thundered past on a distant track, shaking dust loose from the roof beams. People looked up hopefully, then returned to waiting.

She checked her watch again.

That tiny gesture decided everything.

Before I could reconsider, I stood up.

My legs carried me toward her while the rest of me tried to retreat. I stopped beside her—close enough to speak, close enough to hear my own breathing, close enough to realize I had made a terrible mistake.

She still had not noticed me.

My prepared sentences vanished.

My throat tightened.

I stood there in silence for what felt like years and was probably less than ten seconds.

Then she turned.

Her eyes met mine briefly, curious but calm.

And because panic is often more honest than courage, the first words that escaped me were:

“Do you know what time the Vishakha Express arrives here?”

For a moment she simply stared.

Then one corner of her mouth lifted.

“Really?” she said. “You came all the way here to ask me that?”

The station noise faded.

The winter air changed.

And without knowing it, my life had just begun to divide itself into two parts—

before that question,

and after it.
