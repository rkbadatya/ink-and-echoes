Some do it with discipline. Some with silence. And some with a strange sense of humor that pretends to be tradition.

Ours chose cricket.

It started one ordinary evening during break, when a few seniors gathered near the corridor and casually asked who among the juniors played cricket. Names were thrown around quickly. Confident names. Loud names. The kind that belong to people who have never actually been tested.

Mine was not mentioned.

I stayed silent, relieved.

That relief did not last long.

A senior's voice cut through the group.

"You. Come."

For a moment I looked behind me, hoping it was directed at someone more athletic, more suitable, more visible.

It wasn't.

By the time I reached the ground, the decision had already been made. We were no longer students in a casual evening break. We were "teams," arranged by senior convenience, standing on a field that suddenly felt much larger than it actually was.

The seniors had already claimed the space with ease. Their laughter carried the confidence of people who did not need permission to enjoy anything.

A bat was handed to me.

It felt unfamiliar in my hands, like something borrowed from another life.

"Open the innings," someone announced.

There was applause, exaggerated and deliberate, as if I had been chosen for something far more dramatic than cricket.

I walked to the crease.

The bowler took his mark slowly, enjoying the moment. He did not need pace or precision. He only needed audience participation, and the audience was fully available.

The first ball arrived faster than my confidence.

I reacted late, then earlier, then not at all. The bat moved like a confused object in search of meaning. The ball passed somewhere behind me, followed by loud appeals that seemed more theatrical than serious.

"Good leave!" someone shouted.

Laughter followed immediately.

The second delivery hit my pad.

More noise. More commentary. No clarity.

The third ball finally brushed the bat and rolled away weakly. It was not a shot. It was an accident with direction.

Still, someone shouted, "Run!"

I did not.

There are moments when silence feels safer than movement.

Then, unexpectedly, one clean connection happened.

The ball went past mid-on—not with power, not with style, but with enough intention to confuse everyone watching.

For half a second, there was silence.

Then the field exploded into reactions.

"Arre, scholar can play!"

"Natural talent!"

"Select him for college team!"

The same voices that had built the joke now expanded it into praise. It was never about truth. It was about momentum.

My innings ended soon after, on a shot that tried to prove confidence before earning it.

As I walked back, one of the seniors patted my shoulder lightly.

"Not bad," he said. "But studies suit you more."

For once, I had no argument.

What followed in the next days was not cruelty, not exactly. It was a collection of rituals disguised as fun—fielding drills that made no sense, forced commentary on every mistake, impossible catches thrown just to watch failure unfold.

We were not being broken.

We were being observed.

And slowly, something shifted.

The seniors stopped feeling like authority figures carved from distance. They became people who laughed too loudly, got bored too quickly, and needed these games more than we needed approval.

Even among juniors, something loosened. The shared embarrassment turned into shared laughter. Conversations started where silence used to sit.

It turned out fear is heavy, but laughter distributes weight better.

I avoided cricket after that as much as possible. Whenever a bat appeared, I developed urgent academic obligations somewhere else.

But something had already changed.

The ground no longer felt like a test.

And I was no longer only the boy who stood in the back with books.

Just once, accidentally, I had hit a boundary.

And somehow, that was enough to give me a story I would be teased about far longer than the shot itself ever deserved.
