I spent the afternoon settling into my brother's room.

Everything there had its place. Books stood in neat alignment, clothes were folded with precision, and even the smallest things seemed disciplined. It was the kind of order that silently made comparisons inevitable in a family. In ours, my brother was always that comparison.

My mother often spoke of him as the example.

"See how he keeps everything clean."

"See how responsible he is."

"Learn from him."

My younger brother, Akhilesh, used to protest in his own way.

"If he is the perfect one, why did you even need us?"

I usually said nothing. Staying out of it felt safer.

After a while, I grew restless inside the room. I had finished arranging what little I had brought, and there was nothing left to keep me occupied. So I stepped outside.

The world outside Mandiapalli felt unexpectedly calm.

Despite being part of a city, it still carried traces of something quieter—open patches of land, slow-moving wind, and a kind of silence that didn't feel empty, just unhurried. I stood there for a moment without thinking about anything. For the first time since arriving, I felt unburdened.

No one here knew me.

No expectations followed me.

It felt like I could quietly become someone new.

I took out the bicycle, mounted it, and began riding without any destination in mind.

The roads slowly widened into unfamiliar parts of the city. What started as aimless wandering turned into curiosity taking control. I crossed intersections whose names I barely registered at the time—State Bank Square, ITI Square, Medical Campus, Annapurna Market.

Everything felt larger than the world I had grown up in. Louder. Faster. More alive.

At Annapurna Market, I stopped near a small juice shop. Without much thought, I bought an ice cream, a couple of chocolates, and a glass of juice. It felt like spending without reason, like briefly stepping outside responsibility.

As I stood there, a motorcycle rushed past far too close. The sudden wind brushed against me and left behind a moment of discomfort I ignored too quickly.

The market itself was overwhelming. Shops pressed tightly against each other, signboards glowing, people moving in every direction, cycles and scooters weaving through impossible gaps. It felt less like a street and more like a living organism refusing to slow down.

I started riding again, more cautiously this time.

A slow-moving rickshaw appeared ahead of me, and I found myself stuck behind it. Every attempt to overtake failed as oncoming traffic forced me back again and again. Then, almost effortlessly, a small boy on a cycle passed both of us without hesitation.

Something in me reacted before I thought.

I sped up.

That small decision changed everything.

At the exact moment I tried to overtake, my phone rang. I glanced down instinctively.

When I looked up again, a car was already too close.

There was no time to correct anything.

I swerved back, but the impact had already begun. The car hit the rear of my bicycle, twisting the handle violently. I lost control and crashed into a parked bike. The force threw me sideways, and my face struck a concrete pillar before everything went dark.

I don't know how long I stayed unconscious.

When I opened my eyes, I was in a hospital bed.

Pain arrived before awareness did. My head throbbed heavily, my left leg burned, and my mouth felt unfamiliar. When I touched my lips, I realized several teeth were broken. My forehead and nose were bandaged, and stitches pulled slightly whenever I moved.

A nurse entered and noticed I was awake.

"How did I get here?" I asked weakly.

"A young woman brought you," she replied. "She went to get medicines."

That line stayed with me.

A young woman.

Before I could process it fully, exhaustion pulled me back into sleep.

In that half-dream state, I saw fragments of home—my father's silence, my mother's voice, my brother sitting nearby. Everything familiar felt distant, like a life I had stepped out of too quickly.

Then I felt a gentle touch.

When I opened my eyes again, she was there.

Alisha Di sat beside the bed, her hand resting lightly on mine. A small bag of medicines and fruit was placed beside her. She looked the same as she had on the bus, except now her expression carried worry instead of ease.

"You're awake," she said softly. "Are you feeling better?"

"Yes," I managed.

She studied me for a moment, then asked, "Do you want the doctor?"

"No, Di… I'm fine."

My voice cracked slightly, and I couldn't hold back the question.

"How did you know?"

She smiled faintly, as if she had been waiting for that question.

"I called you after reaching home," she said. "You didn't pick up. Later, someone answered from your phone and told me about the accident."

She had come immediately after that.

A stranger had helped move me to the roadside. From there, she arranged everything—getting me onto her scooter, holding me steady through traffic, bringing me to the hospital. She had even contacted my brother herself.

"I didn't call your parents," she added. "There was no need to worry them for something like this."

Something like this.

I almost laughed, but even that hurt too much.

Instead, something heavier rose inside me, and I turned my face away as tears slipped out without permission.

She noticed immediately. Without hesitation, she wiped them gently with her dupatta.

"Do I not have the right," she asked quietly, "to take care of my little brother?"

I couldn't answer.

Not because I didn't want to—but because something inside me had already accepted it.

She fed me slowly, making sure I ate and drank. Not long after, my brother arrived, panic still visible on his face. Before he could say anything harsh, she explained everything to him calmly.

His anger faded.

Then he looked at me properly for the first time and noticed my mouth.

"What happened to your teeth?"

I gave him a tired look.

He grinned despite everything. "Now I will call you Chandra Danta."

Moon-toothed.

I didn't respond, but from that day onward, I became conscious of my smile in a way I never had before.

Later, my brother went to speak with the doctor.

For the first time, I noticed how quiet the room had become.

"Which hospital is this?" I asked her.

"City Hospital," she said. "Near State Bank Square."

"I didn't even notice."

"You were too busy meeting accidents," she replied lightly.

I smiled faintly, then winced at the pain.

She immediately leaned forward. "Headache?"

I nodded.

Without saying anything, she gently lifted my head and placed it on her lap. Then, with careful fingers, she began massaging my forehead.

The pain slowly softened.

The room grew quieter than before.

And in that silence, something shifted inside me without explanation.

I was no longer just a boy in a hospital bed.

I had found someone who treated me like family.

And I didn't even know when that had happened.
