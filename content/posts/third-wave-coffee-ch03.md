After the first game, life returned to normal again.

Work. Sleep. Meetings. Notifications. Traffic. Random weekends disappearing without permission.

But somewhere in the middle of all that, Vaishnavi had quietly entered my routine.

Not in any dramatic way.

We still barely knew each other. We weren’t constantly texting. We weren’t sharing secrets or talking late into the night.

And yet, I found myself checking my phone more often than usual.

Just in case.

A day or two after the game, we were discussing another booking.

Somewhere in the conversation, I casually told her she could bring her husband along too. Maybe both of them could play singles while I booked another court.

I don’t even know why I said _husband_ so confidently.

Maybe because my brain had already completed the unfinished picture from that evening. The guy waiting near the court. The comfort between them. The Thar parked outside. The way she had said, “Let’s go.”

It all looked settled.

Certain.

She replied quickly.

“He’s not my husband.”

That was it.

No explanation after that.

No:

“He’s my boyfriend.”

No:

“He’s just a friend.”

Nothing.

Just one correction.

And somehow, that single missing sentence stayed in my head longer than it should have.

Because the human mind is dangerous when given incomplete information.

Especially a lonely mind.

A clear answer closes doors. Ambiguous answers leave them slightly open, and once a door remains even slightly open, imagination quietly walks in and makes itself comfortable.

I remember staring at her message for longer than necessary.

Trying to act rational.

Trying not to read meaning into ordinary words.

Maybe she simply answered the exact thing I asked.

Maybe the rest didn’t matter to her.

Maybe I was just another badminton contact in her phone.

But another part of me — the foolish part — kept replaying the sentence differently.

_Not husband._

Only that.

And unfortunately, hope does not need certainty to survive.

Sometimes ambiguity alone is enough.

The next week, she booked the court herself.

I noticed that immediately.

It sounds small, but effort becomes visible when you secretly care about someone. Until then, every interaction had felt accidental. Temporary. Like two people randomly crossing paths because of an app.

But her booking the game herself made it feel slightly different.

Slightly mutual.

While driving toward the venue that evening, I noticed her walking on the side of the road some distance ahead. Maybe three hundred meters away from the court.

For a second I slowed down automatically.

Then I honked lightly so she would notice.

She turned.

I rolled down the window and asked if she wanted to join.

She smiled politely and said she would rather walk.

That was all.

A normal answer.

A completely reasonable answer.

Yet the rejection landed more sharply inside me than it should have.

Maybe because somewhere in my head, offering someone a lift belonged to a certain category of closeness. A quiet comfort between people.

Nidhi had never refused when I offered.

But Vaishnavi was not Nidhi.

And that realization mattered.

Nidhi and I had years of familiarity behind our comfort. Shared history naturally removes hesitation. Vaishnavi barely knew me outside badminton courts and short conversations.

To her, refusing the lift was probably the most ordinary thing in the world.

But attraction has a dangerous habit of treating ordinary boundaries like personal disappointments.

So I nodded casually, drove ahead, parked near the venue, and acted like the moment had not affected me at all.

Even though it had.

So we played again.

This time the awkwardness was lighter.

Not gone.

Just reduced enough for silence to become comfortable instead of heavy.

We still didn’t talk much outside the game. Most conversations remained about badminton itself. Timing. Courts. Missed shots. Whether the shuttle was out.

But familiarity had started forming quietly between those small exchanges.

And somewhere between those two games, something dangerous began happening inside me.

I started looking forward to her presence more than the sport itself.

That realization arrived slowly.

Not through jealousy.

Not through love.

Not through some cinematic moment.

Just through repeated possibilities.

Through seeing her name appear on my screen and feeling my mood improve slightly without permission.

Through checking Playo hoping she had booked something.

Through ordinary moments gaining unnecessary emotional weight.

And maybe that is how attachment truly begins.

Not with confession.

Not with certainty.

But with attention slowly becoming habit.
