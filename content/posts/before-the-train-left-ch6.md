There are searches made with logic.

And there are searches made with desperation.

Mine belonged entirely to the second kind.

The room was dark except for the pale glow of the monitor. Everyone else in the house had gone to sleep. Doors were shut. Lights were off. Somewhere a ceiling fan turned with the soft rhythm of late-night domestic peace.

Only I remained awake, conducting an investigation with almost no evidence.

Name: Asmita.

Possible college: Vignan.

Possible city: Berhampur.

Possible train traveler.

Possible destroyer of mental balance.

I typed combinations into the search bar like a man trying passwords to his own future.

**Asmita Berhampur**

**Asmita Vignan**

**Asmita Priya**

**Asmita + mutual friends**

**Asmita Orissa**

Every result brought fresh hope for two seconds and disappointment immediately after.

One profile belonged to a schoolteacher twice my age.

Another had no picture, no posts, no visible life.

One was clearly fake—its profile image was a movie actress.

Another seemed promising until I opened the photos and found three children calling her *Mummy* in the comments.

I rubbed my eyes.

“This is impossible.”

Yet I continued.

There is a point in certain obsessions where failure only increases effort.

At 12:40 AM I found a profile with a baby girl as the display picture.

I stopped.

The child wore a light-colored frock and made a mischievous face at the camera. The account name read:

**Cute Princess Asmita (Priya)**

My heartbeat changed.

It was ridiculous to react to a profile named like that. Still, something about it felt playful enough to belong to her.

I opened it.

Most information was hidden.

No recent posts.

No public photos except the same baby picture.

Few visible friends.

No clear college listed.

I scrolled carefully as if hidden truth might appear lower on the page.

Then I found one small clue.

Among mutuals was someone from NIST.

I frowned.

She had said Vignan.

Maybe not her.

Maybe a fake story.

Maybe coincidence.

Maybe nothing.

The internet is cruel because it offers just enough possibility to keep you trapped.

I clicked through the friend list I could see. Random names. No certainty. No useful trail.

I leaned back in the chair, staring at the screen.

Could she have lied?

The thought came softly, then louder.

Had the entire station conversation been entertainment for her? A bored traveler amusing herself before boarding a train?

No.

Something in me resisted that conclusion.

Her expressions had felt real. Her pauses had felt real. The way she spoke about painting had not sounded invented.

And yet, I knew almost nothing.

At 1:05 AM I closed the browser in frustration.

The sudden silence of the desktop room felt heavier than the noise of searching.

I stood and walked to the window.

The street outside was empty. A dog slept near the electric pole. The same streetlight flickered at intervals, as if unable to commit fully to brightness.

I should have gone to bed.

Instead, I returned to the desk, opened my diary, and uncapped a pen.

Some emotions arrive before language knows what to do with them. On those nights, people either cry, call someone, or write badly.

I chose the third option.

At the top of the page I wrote:

**For the mysterious girl I met today**

Then, after thinking far too seriously, I began:

*Oh dear, oh dear,*
*How did you leave me here?*

*You smiled once and boarded away,*
*Now my thoughts refuse to stay.*

I stopped and groaned.

“This is terrible.”

Still, I continued.

*If names were enough to find a face,*
*I’d search the world at double pace.*

*If trains returned what they took tonight,*
*I’d wait till dawn beneath that light.*

I put the pen down.

It was embarrassing.

It was sincere.

That made it dangerous.

I closed the diary quickly as if someone might read my thoughts through the door.

The clock showed 1:32 AM.

I finally shut down the computer and lay on the bed.

This time sleep came in fragments.

I dreamt of train platforms where every girl turned away before I could see her face.

I dreamt of typing Asmita forever into an endless search bar.

I dreamt of hearing my own name spoken once, then losing the voice in station noise.

Morning arrived too soon.

At 8:30 AM I woke and checked Facebook before brushing my teeth.

One new friend request.

My pulse jumped.

I clicked immediately.

**Deepak Sharma**

I stared at the screen with profound disappointment.

Nothing against Deepak Sharma.

But he was not the future I had hoped for.

By noon I checked again.

Nothing.

By afternoon.

Nothing.

By evening.

Still nothing.

I tried to act normal around the family, but my attention had been leased to the notification icon.

At 8:10 PM a message notification appeared.

I opened it quickly.

A friend had sent a New Year party plan.

I replied without reading.

Closed the tab.

Reopened Facebook.

Still nothing.

I laughed at myself.

One conversation at a station, and now I was measuring time through notifications.

By night I sat online again, pretending to chat casually with friends.

At 10:15 PM a friend request popped up.

Without thinking, I clicked **Confirm** and returned to another chat window.

Ten minutes later, a new message appeared.

**hey**

I glanced at the sender name.

**Cute Princess Asmita (Priya)**

For one full second, my body forgot how to move.
