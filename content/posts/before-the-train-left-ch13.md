Some victories last only until the next notification.

The morning after I unfriended her, I woke with the stale pride of someone who had acted dramatically in the dark and now had to live with daylight.

For the first few minutes, I felt justified.

She had ignored me.
She had reduced everything to “chats.”
She had made me feel foolish.

I had responded.

Cleanly. Finally.

Then I opened Facebook.

One message waited.

From her.

Even after unfriending, the thread still existed.

**Can you listen once before judging me?**

I stared at the sentence.

Anger advised me to ignore it.

Curiosity opened the chat.

## The Return

**What now?** I typed.

She replied quickly, as if she had been waiting.

**I deserved your anger. But not your misunderstanding.**

I almost scoffed.

**Interesting line. Continue.**

There was a pause.

Then:

**I lied to you at the station.**

I read it twice.

My chest tightened.

**About what?**

**About almost everything.**

The room became silent again.

The station returned to me in fragments—her voice, her sad story, the trapped daughter, the forced marriage, the painter fighting the world.

All the details I had admired.

All the vulnerability that had made me care.

My fingers moved stiffly.

**Say clearly.**

She began typing in bursts.

**I was not leaving home forever.**
**No one was forcing my marriage.**
**I was going to my uncle’s place.**
**I do like painting, but not like I told you.**
**I made the story up.**

I leaned back from the screen as if distance could reduce impact.

## The Shock

There are truths that hurt because they are cruel.

And truths that hurt because they expose how willingly we believed.

I had listened to every word that day with full sincerity.

I had admired her courage.

I had felt protective.

I had thought fate had introduced me to someone extraordinary.

Now it seemed fate had introduced me to imagination.

**Why?**

That was all I could type.

She answered slowly.

**Because you were a stranger.**

Then:

**Because I was nervous.**

Then:

**Because I wanted to see what kind of person you were.**

I laughed aloud once, without humor.

A test.

My sleepless nights had been a test.

## Her Side

She continued before I could reply.

**You came suddenly and started talking. I didn’t know if you were genuine or just passing time.**

**So you created a drama?**

**I created distance.**

**By telling lies?**

**By telling a story.**

Even then she chose words carefully.

That irritated me more.

**Do you know what happened to me after that day?**

**I know some of it.**

**No, you don’t.**

And suddenly everything I had hidden came out.

The waiting.

The searches.

The messages.

The humiliation of “seen.”

The nights staring at old chats.

The New Year bitterness.

I typed faster than thought.

Sentence after sentence.

Months of emotion compressed into minutes.

When I finished, there was a long pause.

Then she wrote:

**I didn’t know it was that deep for you.**

That sentence wounded me more than accusation could have.

Because it meant I had suffered in a private world she never fully saw.

## The Other Confession

Then came the line that changed the conversation.

**But I searched for you too.**

I stopped.

**What?**

She replied:

**After the train left, I kept thinking about you.**

**I thought I’d forget in an hour. I didn’t.**

**I searched your name from what I saw on your ID card.**

**I hesitated before sending the request.**

The room felt unstable.

So while I had spent the night obsessing over a stranger—

she had been doing the same.

Different methods. Same restlessness.

**Then why lie later too?**

Her answer came slower now.

**Because by then I liked talking to you.**

**And the longer I delayed truth, the harder it became to tell it.**

That was the most honest thing she had said since I knew her.

## Fever

She added one more detail unexpectedly.

**I had fever that day.**

**What?**

**At the station. In the train too. I was impatient because I was unwell. You thought it was attitude.**

I remembered her hurriedness, her watch-checking, her shifting expressions.

I had interpreted everything romantically or personally.

Sometimes people are not mysterious.

They are simply tired, sick, confused, human.

## The Softening

My anger did not vanish.

But it changed shape.

It became mixed with sadness, understanding, and the uncomfortable realization that neither of us had behaved cleanly.

She had lied.

I had idealized.

She had withdrawn.

I had obsessed.

We were both young enough to mistake emotion for clarity.

## The Last Line of the Night

After a long silence, she wrote:

**Can you forgive one foolish girl?**

I stared at the blinking cursor.

I wanted to say no.

I wanted to say yes.

I wanted to ask whether forgiveness would change anything.

Instead I wrote:

**I don’t know yet.**

She replied:

**Fair answer. Goodnight.**

Then she went offline.

I sat there much longer than necessary.

The girl from the station had not turned into a villain.

She had turned into something more difficult:

real.
