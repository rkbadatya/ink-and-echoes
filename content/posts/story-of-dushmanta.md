The Night It Began

The lodge stood still and silent as we finally arrived—its dim yellow lights flickering like they were hiding secrets. None of us spoke. Our breaths were shallow, heavy with shock. What had just happened didn’t feel real.

It was, without question, the most terrifying night of our lives.

Aman was still bleeding. The sleeve of his shirt was soaked, clinging to his arm like a second skin. The rest of us had scratches and bruises, but his injury was serious. He didn’t utter a word, just walked ahead with clenched fists and a burning look in his eyes.

Room 114. That’s where Aman, Abheek, and Shreyash were staying. Ayaan, Deepankar, and I were two doors down in Room 110. Close enough to move back and forth barefoot. Close enough for panic to travel quickly.

Shreyash was the first to act. He grabbed the phone and called room service, his voice low and urgent. “Send a first aid kit. Immediately.”

I looked at Aman. His eyes weren’t just angry—they were haunted. Whatever he had seen, whatever had happened, it had shaken him to his core. I’d never seen him like that. None of us had.

A sharp buzz echoed in the room—the doorbell. We all froze for a second.

Ayaan moved toward it, slowly, like he was walking through fog. He cracked the door just enough to grab the kit, careful not to expose what was inside. One look from a curious hotel staffer, and we’d have more trouble than we could handle.

As soon as the door clicked shut, we rushed to Aman. He collapsed into the dining chair without protest. His shirt was nearly ripped open, the fabric stiff with blood. His left arm was a mess—deep red gashes ran down to his wrist, and we couldn’t tell how deep they went.

Ayaan popped open the Dettol bottle. The sharp, sterile smell filled the room as he dabbed a cotton ball and reached for Aman’s arm. The moment it touched the wound, Aman winced. His entire body tensed, but he didn’t make a sound. Just stared blankly ahead, jaw tight, eyes stormy.

“We need to stop the bleeding,” Abheek said, voice shaking. He and Shreyash worked quickly, smearing some lotion from the kit and wrapping the wound in gauze as best as they could.

Deepankar grabbed the phone again. “Painkillers. Antibiotics. Now,” he told the front desk, then hung up without waiting for a reply.

Minutes crawled by. The silence in the room was thick, broken only by the occasional hiss of pain or the rustling of bandages. Then—ding-dong.

This time, I answered. My hand hesitated on the handle, unsure of who or what would be on the other side. I opened the door just a little.

The room service guy was there, holding a tray. He leaned in slightly, eyes curious. “Something happened, sir?” he asked, his voice too casual for the weight hanging in the air.

I forced a smile and took the tray. “Nothing. Just...clumsy hands,” I muttered, and shut the door before he could say another word.

But deep down, we all knew—this was just the beginning.

Deepankar took the medicines from my hand, his face pale under the harsh white light. He read the labels slowly, his lips silently mouthing the names, double-checking every detail. Only when he was sure did he hand one tablet and a half-empty water bottle to Aman.

Aman took them without a word, his face blank, distant. The room was too quiet. The hum of the ceiling fan sounded louder than it should have. None of us dared speak about what had just happened. Not yet. Not while the air still felt heavy with something unfinished.

We mumbled a tense “good night” to the others and slipped back into Room 110—me, Ayaan, and Deepankar. But the silence followed us like a shadow.

We cleaned up like we were on autopilot. The cool blast of the AC hit our faces, and we turned on the TV, more out of habit than interest. The India vs. West Indies match was playing—its colors bright, commentators too cheerful. The contrast between the room’s eerie silence and the noise from the TV was jarring.

None of us felt even remotely sleepy. _(Hamari toh neend udd gayi thi.)_

Ayaan tried to lighten the mood, rambling about batting strategies, spin variations, pitch behavior—his voice louder than usual, maybe trying to drown out the unspoken.

But our minds weren’t really in the game. And behind that thin wall, Room 114 was a black box of possibilities. We had no idea what Aman, Abheek, or Shreyash were dealing with—or hiding.

I picked up Deepankar’s phone to scroll and escape. My own phone had no internet, and distraction felt necessary. I opened Opera Mini and started browsing Facebook. He was still logged in.

I scrolled absently until one photo stopped me cold.

A group shot from our college picnic.

Neha stood in the middle.

Something shifted inside the room—like the tension found a new crack to crawl into.

I smirked and nudged Deepankar. “Tell me the truth… you went to that picnic just to see Neha, didn’t you?”

He didn’t laugh. Didn’t even smile.

Instead, something in his expression flickered—like a mask slipping mid-performance. His eyes darkened. His lips pressed into a thin, trembling line.

“Hey… I was just joking,” I said quickly, the air turning colder somehow.

“No, it’s not the joke,” he said, his voice barely above a whisper. “It’s just... I ruined her picnic by showing up. She didn’t seem happy to see me. I shouldn’t have gone.”

The room fell silent again, and this time, it wasn’t just awkward—it was loaded.

“I don’t get it,” I said carefully. “Why would she not want you there? You didn’t do anything wrong.”

He shook his head slowly. “It was all a mistake. She never told me anything directly, of course. For her, I was always just… the nice guy. A good friend.”

There was something else in his voice—regret wrapped in something darker.

“But what was the mistake?” I asked, uneasy now.

“You remember that day?” he said. “She was talking about me to her friends—something she thought I wouldn’t hear. You got up and left because our internal exam was about to start.”

“Yeah. I didn’t like what she said about you,” I replied, memories flashing back like shadows on a screen.

“You did the right thing,” he said. “That’s what a good friend does. But... you also misunderstood her. We both did.”

He leaned forward, elbows on his knees, face lit only by the flickering TV. "It was my fault. I should’ve said something to her earlier. I should’ve been honest. But I couldn’t. I was already too far in. I was in love. She was… my first love.”

That last sentence hung in the air like smoke—visible, choking, and impossible to ignore.

I leaned forward too. “Don’t confuse me, man. Just tell me what really happened.”

Deepankar exhaled, long and slow. His shoulders slumped like he was about to unload years of weight.

“Thik hai yaar,” he said finally, eyes lowered. “Aaj tujhe apni kahani batata hoon... par ek baat—no opinions, no comments. Just listen.”

And in that moment, I knew—whatever he was about to say... it wasn’t going to be simple. Or easy to forget.
