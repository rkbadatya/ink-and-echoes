There is a kind of sadness that does not arrive dramatically.

It does not break doors, overturn furniture, or announce itself through tears.

It sits quietly beside you day after day until your habits begin to resemble it.

That was the week I became the boy who waited.

I waited in the morning before brushing my teeth.

I waited after lunch with the computer already on.

I waited in the evening while pretending to talk with friends.

I waited at night when the house had gone silent and every sound felt louder than it should.

Most of all, I waited for the small green light beside her name.

It is embarrassing now to admit how little was required to control my mood.

One message from her could repair an entire day.

One unread reply could ruin it.

One “seen” could become a personal tragedy.

Youth often mistakes intensity for love and attention for oxygen.

I knew none of this then.

## Life Outside the Screen

People around me noticed changes before I did.

My mother asked why I was eating less.

My father asked why I was spending so much time in my room.

My brother asked no questions at all, which was more dangerous.

He simply watched.

One evening he entered my room without knocking and stood behind me.

“What are you doing?”

“Nothing.”

“You’re staring at Facebook.”

“I’m talking to friends.”

“You haven’t typed for five minutes.”

“I’m thinking.”

“That is the problem,” he said. “Too much thinking.”

He sat on the bed and looked around the room.

Books untouched.

Cricket bat in the corner gathering dust.

Desk cluttered with old papers.

Curtains half closed despite daylight.

“This place looks like someone lost an election.”

“Please leave.”

He ignored that.

“Who is she?”

I froze.

“There is no she.”

“Then there is definitely a she.”

I hated how easily brothers turn denial into confession.

He stood to leave, then paused at the door.

“Whatever this is, don’t disappear into it.”

The sentence annoyed me because it was wise.

## Attempts at Normalcy

The next day I forced myself outside.

I met friends near the tea stall. They joked, argued about cricket, discussed plans, laughed at things that required no emotional investment.

I laughed where appropriate.

But half my attention remained elsewhere.

Was she online now?

Had she replied?

Should I check once discreetly?

Someone asked my opinion about a movie.

I said, “Maybe.”

“There is no maybe,” my friend said. “Have you even listened?”

Apparently not.

I returned home early under the excuse of a headache.

The headache was real, though not physical.

## The Message Drafts

I became skilled at writing messages never sent.

**Hey, did I do something wrong?**

Deleted.

**You could at least reply.**

Deleted.

**If you’re busy, just say so.**

Deleted.

**I miss talking to you.**

Deleted fastest of all.

Pride survives longest in private drafts.

Sometimes I sent something simpler.

**Hi**

**What’s up**

**Busy?**

These received the same answer every unanswered question receives:

silence.

## Memory as Punishment

Without new conversations, old ones grew stronger.

I remembered the station.

Her saying my name.

The train door.

The way she laughed at my nervousness.

Then the early chats.

Her teasing.

Her strange midnight seriousness.

The message where she said she understood me.

I replayed everything until memory stopped feeling sweet and became a form of self-harm.

## The Unfair Truth

I began blaming her for emotions she had not asked me to build.

She had never promised constancy.

Never said we were anything.

Never requested devotion.

Yet I had turned her presence into structure.

And now I resented her for failing to hold up a building I built alone.

At the time, I did not understand this.

At the time, I simply felt wronged.

## New Year Approaches

The year was ending.

People around me became festive.

Plans were made.

Calls increased.

Friends discussed parties, outings, resolutions they would forget by January third.

I participated mechanically.

Inside, I carried the stale air of unfinished conversation.

On the last evening of the year, I opened her profile again.

Same baby picture.

Same hidden world.

Same distance.

I hovered over the **Unfriend** button.

Not because I wanted freedom.

Because I wanted reaction.

I wanted to hurt her absence the way absence had hurt me.

I clicked away without doing it.

Cowardice or mercy—I could not tell.

## Midnight

When the new year began, fireworks sounded somewhere far away.

Messages flooded in.

Friends, cousins, classmates, people I had not spoken to in months suddenly remembered my existence.

I replied to some.

Ignored many.

Then I saw a new message notification.

My heartbeat knew before my eyes did.

It was from her.

**Happy New Year 2014 :)**

I stared at the screen for a long time.

One line.

After all those days.

As if silence had been nothing.

As if I had been waiting in a room she could enter casually whenever she wished.

My anger rose faster than reason.

I did not know then that pain often disguises itself as dignity.

I only knew this:

the boy who had waited all week was gone.

And someone harsher was about to answer.
