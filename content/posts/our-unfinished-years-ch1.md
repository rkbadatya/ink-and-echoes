Being a nerd has its advantages. You do not have to worry much about the world around you, because books become your world. Knowledge feels more important than people, marks more important than memories, and success more important than everything else. But being a nerd has its price too—you often miss the most beautiful parts of life while staring at pages.

That was exactly who I was.

I never imagined life would take me where it eventually did. Neither my parents nor I had planned it. My parents wanted me to become a doctor. To them, it was the safest and most respectable future. But I could never force myself toward that path. The thought of memorizing giant biology books terrified me more than failure itself. I had already grown tired of those subjects during school.

So in my mind, there were only two choices: medicine or technology.

Since medicine felt impossible, I chose the second option almost by instinct. It was not because I had some grand vision for the future. I did not know what engineering truly meant, nor what career awaited me. It was simply fascination mixed with fear. That small decision would eventually lead me to PMEC, to Berhampur, and to the people who would become an inseparable part of my life.

But before that life began, there was another ending I still carried inside me.

I had stood second at the district level.

For everyone else, it was an achievement. For me, it was pain. I had expected to be first. Losing that place by only four marks felt crueler than failure itself. My score was excellent, yet I could not celebrate it. My parents were happy, but not enough to turn the house festive.

There was also a silent rivalry between schools. Every school wanted to claim the district topper as their own. Teachers took pride in their students’ ranks as if they were personal victories. And in my own mind, I had failed my school.

To hide my disappointment, I accompanied my father to visit teachers and well-wishers. Everyone had advice.

“Send him to BJB College in Bhubaneswar.”

“No, let him stay closer to home.”

“He should prepare seriously for engineering.”

“Why not diploma first?”

I listened, but understood little. My diploma entrance rank was ninety-two, but I had never seriously considered that route. I wanted to continue with +2 studies, and most of my teachers agreed.

Finally, after discussions with elders and my Big Bro, the decision was made: I would move to Berhampur, study there, and stay with him.

I did not protest. In those days, children did not question decisions made by elders. They accepted them like weather.

Though many teachers wanted me to remain in Phulbani, my road had already changed direction.

My father had recently been transferred, and one of his colleagues lived in Berhampur. We took the first morning bus and reached the city by around ten. I watched the road in silence, carrying equal amounts of fear and excitement.

At his colleague Mr. Mohapatra’s house, we freshened up and ate a full meal. He had already shortlisted a few colleges and arranged their application forms. I filled them carefully and handed them to my father.

Our first stop was Khalikote Junior College.

I had never seen traffic like that in my life. The noise, the crowd, the movement—it all made me nervous. Berhampur felt too fast, too restless, too alive. I wondered how I would survive in such a place.

After standing in lines for nearly two hours, we finally submitted the form. I was already exhausted and wanted to go home immediately.

But the day had one more stop.

Mr. Mohapatra called an auto-rickshaw and asked the driver to take us to Nilakantha Nagar Saraswati Vidya Mandir.

The moment I entered the campus, something changed inside me.

The surroundings were clean, peaceful, and disciplined. The buildings looked cared for. The pathways were neat. Even the air felt calmer. I had always admired institutions under the Saraswati Vidya Mandir name, and seeing this campus in person erased every other option from my mind.

As I submitted my application form, the clerk looked at me, then at the form, then back at me for a long moment. After handing me the receipt, he suggested that we look around the campus and its facilities.

I glanced at my father. He silently approved.

A man dressed in a white shirt and trousers guided us through the corridors, classrooms, laboratories, and offices. Everything was spotless. It felt less like a college and more like a place that respected learning.

Then we were taken into the principal’s office.

The room smelled faintly of books and polished wood.

He was surprisingly gentle for a man in such authority. He spoke politely, warmly, and with the kind of confidence that makes others trust him immediately. I barely said a word throughout the conversation, but he did not seem bothered by my silence.

When the topic of admission came up, he encouraged my father to complete it quickly.

“We should not let a talented student slip away,” he said with a smile.

That sentence stayed with me.

Back home, when my family asked for my opinion, I answered without hesitation.

I wanted Saraswati Vidya Mandir, Nilakantha Nagar.

And when the admission letter arrived, I did not know it then—but my real story had already begun.
