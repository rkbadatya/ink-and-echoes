First Night in Hyderabad

The lodge stood still and silent as we finally arrived—its dim yellow lights flickering like they were hiding secrets. None of us spoke. Our breaths were shallow, heavy with shock. What had just happened didn’t feel real.

It was, without question, the most terrifying night of our lives.

Aman was still bleeding. The sleeve of his shirt was soaked, clinging to his arm like a second skin. The rest of us had scratches and bruises, but his injury was serious. He didn’t utter a word, just walked ahead with clenched fists and a burning look in his eyes.

Room 114. That’s where Aman, Abheek, and Shreyash were staying. Ayaan, Deepankar, and I were two doors down in Room 110. Close enough to move back and forth barefoot. Close enough for panic to travel quickly.

Shreyash was the first to act. He grabbed the phone and called room service, his voice low and urgent. “Send a first aid kit. Immediately.”

I looked at Aman. His eyes weren’t just angry—they were haunted. Whatever he had seen, whatever had happened, it had shaken him to his core. I’d never seen him like that. None of us had.

A sharp buzz echoed in the room—the doorbell. We all froze for a second.

Ayaan moved toward it, slowly, like he was walking through fog. He cracked the door just enough to grab the kit, careful not to expose what was inside. One look from a curious hotel staffer, and we’d have more trouble than we could handle.

As soon as the door clicked shut, we rushed to Aman. He collapsed into the dining chair without protest. His shirt was nearly ripped open, the fabric stiff with blood. His left arm was a mess—deep red gashes ran down to his wrist, and we couldn’t tell how deep they went.

Ayaan popped open the Dettol bottle. The sharp, sterile smell filled the room as he dabbed a cotton ball and reached for Aman’s arm. The moment it touched the wound, Aman winced. His entire body tensed, but he didn’t make a sound. Just stared blankly ahead, jaw tight, eyes stormy.

“We need to stop the bleeding,” Abheek said, voice shaking. He and Shreyash worked quickly, smearing some lotion from the kit and wrapping the wound in gauze as best as they could.

Deepankar grabbed the phone again. “Painkillers. Antibiotics. Now,” he told the front desk, then hung up without waiting for a reply.

Minutes crawled by. The silence in the room was thick, broken only by the occasional hiss of pain or the rustling of bandages. Then—ding-dong.

This time, I answered. My hand hesitated on the handle, unsure of who or what would be on the other side. I opened the door just a little.

The room service guy was there, holding a tray. He leaned in slightly, eyes curious. “Something happened, sir?” he asked, his voice too casual for the weight hanging in the air.

I forced a smile and took the tray. “Nothing. Just...clumsy hands,” I muttered, and shut the door before he could say another word.

But deep down, we all knew—this was just the beginning.

“No nothing happened. Have you brought the pain killers?” I wanted to end the
conversation as quick as possible.

“Just asking sir. If you need help, call me sir. I am available all the time.”

“Ok” I said and closed the door in his face.

Deepankar took those medicines from my hand and started reading the labels.
After confirming that it was the right medicines he gave Aman one tablet and the
water bottle. Aman took medicines. None of us was in a mood to talk about the
thing just happened. Giving a sigh god night to them we, me, Ayaan and Deepankar
returned to our room. We cleaned ourselves. Switched on the AC as well as TV.
There was the match between India and West Indies was going on. It was the best
distraction could be at that moment. None of us was feeling sleepy. Hamare to
neend udgaye the. 

Ayaan was giving his opinion on how the batsman should bat, how the ball was
turning etc. etc. We had no idea what could be possibly going on the other room.
As I had no internet connection in my mobile, so I picked up Deepankar’s phone
and opened opera mini to browse and of course Facebook. But it was him who had
logged in. I scrolled down his home page and saw our college picnic photo where
Neha was. There was something wrong between him and Neha. So to make fun with
him I showed him the photo and said, “tell me the truth. You went to picnic to
see Neha, na?”

He didn’t answer but his face became weaken. I asked, “what happened? I was just
joking.”

“No it’s not about your joking. It’s that I spoiled her picnic by going there.
She didn’t look happy seeing me there. I shouldn’t have gone.” His voice
diminished.

“I see no point why she don’t like you. Don’t blame yourself for something that
you have no control over. Will you not enjoy if she doesn’t like it?” I said.

“It was all mistake. She never said me anything like that. For her I was always
a good friend.”

“What are you waffling about? How was it your mistake?” I was confused.

“You remember, once she was saying something about me to her friends and you got
up from there as it was internal exam going on?”

“Yes, I didn’t like her saying bad about you.” I said.

“Hmm… that’s what a good friend should have done and you did that. But you also
mistook her. It was all my mistake. But what could have I done? I was in love.
She was my first love.”

“Please don’t confuse me. Tell me in detail.” I was impatient.

“thik hey yaar, ajj me tujhe apni kahani batata hun…. Par koi opinion yar
comment mat karna” his words.
