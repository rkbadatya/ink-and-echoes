Being a nerd has its perks. You get to live in your own little world, where knowledge is everything—not the trending gossip, not the social drama, but the thrill of understanding complex subjects. That was me—deep into books, equations, and theories. But there’s a flipside to that coin: you often miss out on some of the most beautiful experiences life has to offer. And for a long time, I did. Who would've thought that a series of unplanned choices would lead me to where I am today?

I never envisioned this path for myself. Neither did my parents. They had high hopes of seeing me become a doctor, donning a white coat and saving lives. But I had other plans—or maybe just a stubborn refusal to walk a road paved with biology textbooks. Honestly, I’d grown bored of biology since my matriculation days. There were only two real options before me: medicine or something technical. Since the very idea of memorizing the human anatomy felt like a punishment, the technical path won by default.

I wasn’t exactly clear about what lay ahead, but I was sure of one thing—I wouldn’t compromise. That decision eventually led me to PMEC and to a group of people who would go on to mean the world to me. But before I get into that chapter, let me take you back a little.

The sting of standing second in the district still lingers. I was this close to being the top scorer—just four marks short. That tiny gap felt like a deep wound. Sure, my marks were good, but when you're that close to the top, second place doesn’t feel like a victory. My parents were happy, though not overjoyed. My school? Disappointed. There was a silent rivalry among schools, each wanting to claim the district topper. And I had fallen short—not just for myself, but for them.

In an attempt to figure out the next step, I visited some of my well-wishers and teachers with my dad. There were suggestions flying in from every direction: BJB College in Bhubaneswar, other reputed institutions. But my mind was clouded. I had a diploma rank of 92, yet that was never my goal. I was set on doing +2, and so were my teachers. After a long discussion with Big Bro, it was finally decided—I would move to Berhampur and stay with him.

I had no objections. In those days, decisions were mostly made by the elders, and we just followed. Although some of my teachers didn’t want me to leave Phulbani, the choice had already been made.

Dad had just been transferred to a new school, and one of his colleagues lived in Berhampur. So we boarded the first bus out and reached Mr. Mohapatra’s house by 10 a.m. After freshening up and a wholesome meal, he presented us with a list of colleges and a bunch of application forms. I did my best to fill them out and handed them over to Dad.

Our first stop was Khalikote Junior College. The crowd! I had never seen so many people in one place. I felt like a fish out of water—lost in the noise and hustle of city life. After a grueling two-hour wait, I finally managed to submit my form. I was exhausted and had no interest in exploring the city further, but Mr. Mohapatra had other plans. He hailed an auto and off we went to Nilakantha Nagar Saraswati Vidya Mandir.

That ride cost Rs. 50, but the destination left an impression far beyond that fare.

The moment I stepped onto the campus, I was struck by how peaceful and clean it was. The surrounding felt almost sacred. I had always admired the discipline and values associated with Saraswati Vidya Mandir institutions. And standing there, I just knew—I wanted to be part of this.

While submitting my form, I noticed the clerk eyeing both me and the application form for what felt like two long minutes. Finally, as he handed me the receipt, he suggested we take a look around the campus. I glanced at Dad. He nodded.

Soon, a man dressed in a white shirt and trousers—almost saint-like in demeanor—offered to guide us. The corridors were spotless, the rooms and labs pristine, and the entire campus felt as if it were being taken care of like a newborn. I couldn’t ignore it. We were then led into the principal’s office. He greeted us with a warmth that felt rare in someone of his position. He spoke gently, encouragingly, and when the subject of admission came up, he strongly urged us to proceed. He didn’t want a promising student to slip away. That stayed with me.

When we returned home and I was asked for my opinion, I didn’t hesitate for a second—I wanted to join Nilakantha Nagar Saraswati Vidya Mandir. And once the admission letter arrived, that’s exactly what I did.
